01 — The Problem

Every Friday, I see this
problem in person.

Every Friday I volunteer tutoring homeless students in Seattle. When a kid hits a wall in math, their options are brutally limited:

Option A
Stop trying.
Option B
Copy from AI without understanding.
Option C
Afford tutoring.

What most of these students do have — required by their school — is a TI-84 calculator. An underutilized black box sitting in their bag, capable of so much more than arithmetic.

The hardware is already in their hands. The question is whether it can become a learning tool.

02 — What I Built

An AI tutor that runs
completely offline.

The Teaching Calculator doesn't give answers — it asks questions. Like a real tutor: "What's the first step? What formula applies here?"

Raspberry Pi 5 (16GB)
Runs a quantized Qwen2.5-Math-7B model at Q5_K_M precision — 4.1GB RAM, 2–5 tok/s throughput.
Fully Offline
No WiFi required. Works at a shelter, a library, or anywhere students study. Internet is optional, not required.
ESP32 Wireless Bridge
Custom ESP32 firmware bridges the TI-84's I/O link port to the Pi over low-power wireless.
Socratic AI Tutor
Prompted to guide, not solve. Responds to where the student is stuck, not just what the answer is.

04 — Technical Challenges

Where the hard
problems lived.

CHALLENGE 01
Quantization: Fitting 28GB into 4GB

Needed to fit a 7B parameter model (28–32GB full precision) onto a Pi with 16GB RAM. Systematic testing revealed a counterintuitive sweet spot.

Q8_0 Too slow for interactive use
Q4_0 Produces incoherent output
Q4_K_M Fast, but accuracy loss
Q5_K_M ✓ 4.1GB · 2–5 tok/s · 70–80% acc.
Counterintuitive: quantized model runs faster than full precision — bottleneck is memory bandwidth, not compute.
CHALLENGE 02
The Mac-to-Pi Mystery

Identical model file: 80–85% accuracy on MacBook M2, 40% on Pi. Classic hardware-specific bug.

Root cause: Raspberry Pi wasn't using ARM NEON SIMD instructions. Bit-packing errors compounded silently across all 32 transformer layers.

Fix: Recompiled llama.cpp with ARM NEON optimizations:

cmake .. -DLLAMA_NATIVE=ON \ -DLLAMA_NEON=ON
Result: accuracy restored from 40% → 70–80%. A two-flag fix for a 40-point accuracy gap.
CHALLENGE 03
Energy: The 1,137× Problem

The LLM consumed 1,137× more energy per query than SymPy. At that rate, a full battery couldn't sustain a homework session.

LLM: 9.1W × 30s = 273 Ws per query
SymPy: 4.8W × 0.05s = 0.24 Ws per query

Small optimizations compounded:

  • → Query caching saved 15% of computations
  • → 2048 token context window: 30% faster inference
  • → Disabling HDMI/Bluetooth/LEDs saved 0.7W
Cascade architecture: route to cheapest tier first. 45% of queries never touch the LLM.
CHALLENGE 04
Thermal: Keeping the Pi Alive

Under sustained LLM load, the Pi hit 83°C. The CPU throttled from 2.4GHz → 1.8GHz — a 30–40% inference slowdown.

83°C
Throttle temp
65–70°C
Stable w/ cooler

Solution: iUniker aluminum tower cooler + 30mm 5V fan. Validated across 30-minute stress tests.

Next phase: PWM fan control triggered above 72°C for adaptive noise management.

06 — Takeaways

What building this
actually taught me.

01

Constraints drive creativity.

The 16GB RAM limit forced me into quantization. The battery constraint forced the cascade architecture. The best engineering solutions I found came from having no choice but to be clever.

02

Small optimizations compound into large innovations.

Saving 2.15W in aggregate — HDMI off, context tuning, caching — unlocked chain-of-thought reasoning and made NPU integration feasible. No single optimization was dramatic. All of them together were.

03

Engineering is about knowing which corners to cut and which to obsess over.

I obsessed over SIMD flags that recovered 40 accuracy points. I cut a dedicated display to save memory bandwidth. The judgment of where to care deeply is the whole craft.
